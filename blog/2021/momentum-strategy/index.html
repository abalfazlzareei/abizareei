Momentum Profitability

August 23, 2021

This article tries to examine whether retail investors can enjoy the momentum effect. I use different tools in the investment domain to enhance the profitability of Momentum effects for the retail investors.

What is Momentum?

The original study documenting momentum effect (Jegadeesh and Titman, 1993 - cited as of today 12,623 times) show that stocks that has performed well in the medium-term past, six to twelve months, are prone to outperform in the future. Note that what we call momentum is technically known as medium-term momentum. This is because in short-term (a month or a week) or in long-term (months 13 to 21 months), the effect is reversed: good performance in the past relates negatively to the future performance.

Why does momentum exist?

Researchers mostly explain momentum as a behavioral phenomenon. Papers such as Barberis, Shleifer and Vishny (1998) and Hong and Stein (1999) developed behavioral models to explain momentum profitability. They show that momentum arises due to delayed reaction and overreaction to information. Their models also explain the long-term reversal effect as in the long-run, the inefficient momentum prices are corrected. Moreover, rational explanations, offered by Conrad and Kaul (1989) and Lo and McKinlay (1990), argue that momentum effect is a result of cross-sectionally persistent expected returns: Stocks with high (low) expected returns, have relatively high (low) realized returns in the past and also have high (low) average realized returns in the future. However, this model does not explain the long-run reversal effect (other rational explanations, such as conditional CAPM and intertemporal CAPM of Merton (1973) also fail to explain the momentum effect).

How does momentum work?

Formally, MOM is the return of stock i during the last 11 months:

\[MOM_{i,t}=100[\prod_{j=1}^{11}(1+R_{i(t-j)})-1]\]

The momentum investment process works as follows: We are investing monthly and in the begining of each month, we rebalance our portfolio. Let’s say we are standing at time t (for example, March 2021). We compute MOM for all the stocks in the stock market, we sort the stocks by MOM and buy (short) the stocks with the highest (lowest) MOM values.

Retail investor #1. No short-selling

The norm is to build the long and short portfolios by selecting top and bottom 10% of the stocks. However, for a retail investor this can be too many stocks. To get a good idea of how many stocks is needed, I limit the number of stocks in our portfolio from 10 to 200 stocks. In addition, as a retail investor, it is not that straightforward to short the stocks; thus, we only buy the stocks (no shorting).

Retail investor #2. Size of the long portfolios

Another consideration for the retail investors is the micro-cap stocks. Fama and French (2008) argue that equal-weighted portfolios in the anomaly literature capitalize on micro-caps (stocks with market capitalization lower than the 20th percentile of the NYSE) and since these stocks are relatively illiquid, they command higher trading costs. Subsequently, I limit the analysis to the stocks with market capitalization higher than 20% of the NYSE stocks.

Figure 1 shows the cumulative wealth for a value-weighted momentum portfolio. We form portfolios each months using momentum signals and invest in a predefined number of assets (from 10 up to 200 stocks). Clearly, for a momentum strategy to work properly, we need at least 100 stocks in the portfolio. This is lots of work, no? Imagine each month, selling 100 stocks and buying another 100 stocks. In addition, momentum suffers from crashes. Suddenly, it loses and it loses big. As Figure 1 shows, in 2008, 2015, 2016, 2019 and 2020, these crashes happened. Also, it looks like the “pandemic “ period really boost up the momentum profitability!
